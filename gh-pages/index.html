About

The sketches in The Universal Symbol Sketchbook are the raw material of a new collaboratively created visual language. Most sketches were made at public design events called Iconathons, IconCamps and IconSalons. Our shared goal is to generate symbols that reflect the world we live in -- and the world as we would like it to be.

Final vector symbols created from these sketches are shared with a Public Domain license, to be freely used and adapted by anyone who needs them.

We have created this online gallery to make it easier for everyone to see all of the great ideas for new symbols from around the world. What's special about this design movement is that we have the opportunity to craft new symbols for our world -- and for the first time in history, we can make this process open, participatory, transparent and democratic.

Design New Icons!

Most of the sketches you see in The Universal Symbol Sketchbook were created at collaborative design brainstorms called Iconathons, IconCamps or IconSalons.

Process

We invite communities and community leaders to suggest ideas for new symbols.

We create a short-list of symbols to bring to a community design event:

  • Universal symbols for the idea do not already exist.
  • Symbols will have broad impact if created and shared.
  • Symbols are fun to create (with volunteer time.)
  • Symbols present a design challenge, or require public dialogue to come up with good symbolism for challenging ideas.

We invite designers, citizens and knowledgable people to gather to brainstorm visual ideas. We use paper and markers. We then document all of the sketches with a camera, so that everyone in the world will be able to see what we are doing. A handful of volunteer illustrators will work to digitize ("vectorize") some of the more readable symbols.

Due to volunteer capacity and time, there are usually a lot of great ideas that do not get vectorized, which is why they are online -- so that someone else can take a turn at creating the symbol.

The final vectorized symbols may be contributed and published on the Noun Project website (for example, look at the Iconathon collection). The Noun Project website has a lot of visibility among designers already, and this helps get the new symbol out to the world. Everyone who has worked on the symbol gets attributed, and the symbol is shared as a consistenly formatted scalable vector graphic (SVG) with a Public Domain license.

If your vector symbol is still not finished, but you want to share it, you may contribute it to the Universal Symbol Sketchbook.

Not all new vector symbols are perfect. Technically, a symbol is not "universal" until it is actually used by real people to communicate, but we are getting this process started. Got to start somewhere, right?

We test that a symbol is readable by showing it to someone who has not seen it and asking "What is it?" -- if they can guess, then that's at least the beginning of a good test to see if the new symbol makes sense. If not, we should keep trying to make a better symbol!

IconCamps

Process documented by Chach Sikes (Code for America), Karla Macedo (Code for America) and Sofya Polyakov (The Noun Project) - August 2011

Overview

An Icon Camp is an independently organized collaborative design event in which participants create new civic symbols for the public domain.

Organize an IconCamp

Hosting your own Icon Camp means you get to choose a civic theme you are passionate about. Once you select a civic theme, determine which concepts need to be defined through symbols. Develop a list of symbols that are missing from our visual civic vocabulary, then narrow down the list to a manageable size depending on the number of people you expect to attend your Icon Camp.

If possible, partner with an organization or experts in the field of your theme to do a brief presentation at the event and to help you further define what challenges can be addressed through the use of these newly designed symbols. Then invite talented people passionate about bringing new civic symbols into the world to help you create a new symbol suite for your theme! The symbols you create can be submitted for curation to The Noun Project, and if they meet the stylistic and technical requirements, will be released into the public domain for everyone to use.

About Icon Camps

Icon Camps are the open & independent version the Iconathon (facilitated by the Noun Project.) The goal of all of these events is to generate and release new Civic Symbol Suites for the public domain. The original Iconathon was founded by Code for America and Noun Project with the participation of additional local and national partners.

Tips

Below are some helpful guidelines for what to do before, during, and after your Icon Camp. Keep in mind that each event will have different needs based on your group size. All examples and references are provided via links below. All signs and forms you will need to use during your event can be downloaded from here.

Step 1. Before the Event

Planning
  1. Select a Civic Theme you’re passionate about (ex: women’s health).
  2. Think of a list of 30-50 concepts that need to be visually defined. If you like, you can use the Symbols gallery on the Iconathon website as a resource.
  3. Contact organizations or experts in the field to speak at your event, and provide an overview of which symbols would be most useful. For example, for the Iconathon tour, we invited city employees, urban planners, domain experts, open government advocates to advise us in our list of symbols that would have high impact, to answer questions about the use cases & contexts in which symbols would be used, and to educate & inspire event attendees in the subject matter.
  4. Narrow down the concept list to about 30 nouns, depending on your group size. Example of a List in Progress, Example of final printed Concept List. We recommend one noun for every person attending the event. We found it was helpful to research symbols ahead of time, and to have the symbol list confirmed at least one week prior to the event.
  5. Book a venue or space. Since this is a design event, make sure the space has the necessary amenities for drawing (such as tables and chairs). The space should also have Internet available for researching concepts or images.
  6. Post your event on iconathon.org.
Organizing
  1. Invite designers and non-designers to participate (non-designers provide valuable opinions about the effectiveness of the designs being created). See blog post “But I Can’t Draw”.
  2. Consider using Social Media, such as Eventbrite, Twitter & Facebook, to help spread the word.
  3. Create Folders for each concept you want defined. These are very useful in keeping track of who worked on each symbol, and the sketches created. They’re also a great tool for post-event follow up. Include an Attribution List in each folder. Folder examples: Example 1, Example 2, Example 3.
  4. Create a Day-of-Information handout for your attendees (for larger groups). These can include a brief Day-of-Information Handout, Schedule of the Day.
  5. Consider providing Nametags for participants to encourage communication and networking.
  6. Remind your attendees of the event a day or two before the event, and encourage them to bring laptops and whatever art supplies they would like to use or share.
  7. If possible, arrange to have water, coffee and snacks for attendees.
  8. If you are having presentations, ensure that your venue has a projector and appropriate cables to connect presenter’s laptops.
Essential Art Supplies to have during the event
  • Paper: 8x11 and/or legal size
  • Post-it notes (3”x3” size)
  • Tracing Paper
  • Pencils and lots of Sharpies
  • Scotch tape
  • If your venue has whiteboards, make sure you have dry erase markers.
  • Digital cameras

Step 2. Day of the Event

  1. Arrive about an hour in advance to Set Up the Art Supplies, Folders, Day-of-Info.
  2. Start the event by providing an overview of what you would like to accomplish, and introduce any of your speakers.
  3. If possible, make available the “User Comprehension & How It Applies to Symbol Design” presentation to provide a good overview of best symbol design practices. Coming soon!
  4. Participants should form Groups of 3 to 5 to work on designing symbols as a team. This encourages discussions of the concept and helps narrow down the essence that should be used for symbol design.
  5. We found that the brainstorming process is intensive and people can brainstorm for about 2 hours before they start to get tired.
  6. End the day with Group Presentations about which symbols were designed, what design challenges each group faced, and an overview of which symbols where successful in communicating their intention.
  7. This process can help to clarify which symbols are ready to be converted to graphic by volunteer illustrators. We found that sketches are either very strong and ready to be “vectorized,” or else they “need work.” Not every symbol has to be vectorized. (YouTube videos of group presentations
  8. For large groups, having a strong facilitator who is knowledgeable in art critique process can help move people through group critiques. Critiquing 20 designs takes about an hour.
  9. For small groups without strong design leadership, and in limited time situations, we found that placing small sticker dots on designs that resonated helped to clarify which designs were ready for vectorization.
  10. Make sure that everyone from each group listed their names in the Folder’s Attribution List. It’s important to keep track of who worked on each symbol for attribution purposes. If possible, place a small sticker dot next to symbols that everyone agreed was ready to move on. After the event, it is easy to forget which symbol was the final choice. For an event with 30 attendees, you can easily have 200 sketches generated.
  11. Ask for volunteers who will be in charge of turning the Finished Sketches into Vectorized Symbols. Your volunteers should be graphic designers who are comfortable with Illustrator or other design programs. Write down their emails for further communication.

Step 3. After the Event

  • Reach out to your volunteers to make sure everyone knows what symbol they’re responsible for designing. We recommend a two-week deadline for turning sketches into symbols that are ready to be submitted.
  • Photo-document all of the sketches. Documenting makes managing volunteers easier, and allows other people to see your sketches, which helps other to understand what Iconathons & Icon Camps are all about. The sketches are inspiring, since they tend to be rough, which encourages non-designers to participate.
  • Documenting the sketches from the event takes a few hours, and is easiest done right after the event while you still remember. We found it helpful to photograph sketches with a label & dot next to the symbols that people agreed was final.
Our workflow
  • Upload photos to a Flickr account (if you have one.) In the batch organizer, go through and label all of the photos. Tag all of the photos with the following tags: iconcamp, iconathon, {city name}, {topic}, {organizers}. Send photos to the Iconathon Flickr pool.
  • To share your sketches in the Social Symbol Gallery on the iconathon.org website, download this sample spreadsheet of symbols. You will want to add the name of the symbol, tag each item, and then grab the HTML code for the images in Flickr (or any other photo sharing tool). To add multiple images, separate each HTML tag with a comma. Add a story about the symbol making process in the ‘body’ field, and a ‘description’ of the symbol.
  • Status: To help keep track of symbols, you can set the Status:
    - ‘Not Started’ - helpful for sharing symbols that are important, but no one worked on at the event.
    - ‘Has Sketches’ - helpful to indicate symbols that people tried to make, but did not succeed at the Icon Camp event.
    - ‘Being Vectorized.’ - helpful to indicate that a volunteer has ‘checked out’ the symbol.
  • Volunteers, volunteer email & team. Record the Team for attribution, and emails for keeping track of who is doing what. You will need this information when you submit final symbols to the Noun Project library.
  • To have all of your symbols added, contact @iconathon, iconathon@codeforamerica.org, we will review & upload your spreadsheet to the iconathon.org website if it meets our requirements (technical, subject matter, appropriate.)
  • When your final SVG symbols are in the Noun Project website, we can update the iconathon.org website to show the final sketches. You are welcome to add comments & share SVG versions of symbols on the iconathon.org website. Contact us if you need help: @iconathon, iconathon@codeforamerica.org.
  • For motivation, you might want to arrange for a high profile blog post showcasing the symbols created through the Icon Camp. Tweet your blog post to @Iconathon, @nounproject & @codeforamerica. (Example: Iconathon creates first collaboratively developed Civic Symbol Suite. ( Coming soon!)
  • Make sure the symbols are designed following The Noun Project’s Stylistic and Technical Requirements.
  • You may need to send reminder emails to your volunteer illustrators, and give them some feedback to ensure that those symbols are balanced, have good use of line weight. Remember that everyone is a volunteer, and that people are learning symbol design.
  • User comprehension. A trick for testing that a symbol has good comprehension is to show someone the symbol and ask them what it is. The more people that agree, the better. Especially people from different backgrounds and in different contexts.

    What's the difference between an Iconathon and an Icon Camp?

    Iconathon
    A multi-city design marathon in which many people come together to create symbols based on themes, which are released to the public domain through the Noun Project website as a Civic Symbol Suite after the tour is complete and the design sketches are finished through volunteer effort. Organized by Code for America & The Noun Project. The first Iconathon was a 6 city tour, with 6 different themes. The Civic Symbol Suite will be released in early October 2011. 150 people attended events across the nation, over 100 symbols sketches were generated, and we expect the Civic Symbol Suite to have between 50 and 80 polished symbols. These events will need to be seasonal because the coordination, event planning, and community managing of dozens of volunteer illustrators is time intensive. Code for America is a fellowship program, which means that this years event organizers, Karla Macedo & Chach Sikes, will be somewhere else, though they hope to be involved in next year's Iconathons with more Code for America fellows.
    Icon Camp
    An independently organized event in which a group of people get together to create symbols for the public domain. Symbols can be shared through this website and through the Noun Project's user submission. We are working to provide tools to help the symbols at these events have a broader audience & impact. Those tools include: through event documentation, tips on identifying symbols that need sketches, lists of symbols that no-one worked on at previous Iconathons, lists of sketches that were difficult and need additional attention, information about symbol design. We can make it possible to list your Icon Camp events on this website.
